## 1. What We Sell (in one breath)

On Forward is a **Forward Deployed Engineering (FDE) firm**. We embed inside a client's business, learn how it actually works, and build custom AI systems and agents around the real operation.

We are **not** a platform. **Not** a template. **Not** a course or a strategy deck. We build custom AI for how a specific business actually runs.

The core motion we sell:

1. **Embed** — our engineers sit with the client's team and watch the real work happen.
2. **Map** — we document how operations actually run (not the org chart, the *actual* workflow).
3. **Build** — we ship custom AI agents and workflows around that real process.

**The wedge that opens the conversation:** *The AI skills gap is the #1 barrier to AI adoption — and we close it.* Most companies try to adopt AI through courses, tools, and strategy decks. That approach fails because it never touches how the business actually operates. We start with the operation, not the technology.

**One-line description for cold outreach:**
> "We embed engineers in your team, map how your operations actually run, and build custom AI agents around the real thing — not a platform you have to figure out yourself."

## 2. Positioning & Differentiation

Every On Forward conversation lives in contrast to four alternatives the buyer is already considering. Know each one cold.

| The buyer is considering... | Their problem with it | Our counter |
|---|---|---|
| **An AI platform / SaaS tool** | Generic. They still have to figure out *how* to apply it to their workflow. The skills gap remains. | We deliver the *applied* outcome, built around their real process — not a tool they have to operationalize themselves. |
| **A course / training program** | Knowledge without artifacts. People learn concepts but nothing in the business changes. | We leave behind working systems, not certificates. |
| **A strategy consultancy** | Slides and a roadmap, then they hand it off. Nothing gets built. | We're engineers who embed and ship. The deliverable is running software, not a deck. |
| **Hiring in-house / DIY** | Slow, expensive, and the talent is scarce — this *is* the skills gap. | We bring the skills now, embedded, and transfer capability as we go. |

**The positioning sentence to memorize:**
> "Strategy firms tell you what to do. Platforms give you a tool and wish you luck. We embed, learn how your business actually works, and build the AI around it."

**Why this matters for the AE:** Your job is rarely to convince someone AI is valuable — they already believe that. Your job is to convince them that *the way they're currently trying to adopt it won't work*, and that an embedded, build-it-around-the-real-process model is the one that does.

## 3. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) & Personas

### Firmographic fit
- **Size:** `[CONFIRM: e.g., mid-market 50–1,000 employees]` — large enough to have real operational complexity and budget, small enough to move fast.
- **Profile:** Operationally complex businesses with repetitive, knowledge-heavy workflows that haven't been automated — operations, finance, support, logistics, claims, back-office, professional services.
- **Trigger signals (good time to buy):** new "AI initiative" mandate from leadership; a failed/stalled AI pilot; rapid growth straining ops; a recent tools purchase that "isn't sticking"; a competitor publicly using AI.

### Buyer personas
- **Economic buyer** — usually a COO, VP Operations, Head of Transformation, or a CEO at smaller firms. Cares about throughput, cost-to-serve, and capacity without headcount.
- **Champion** — an ops or function lead drowning in manual work who feels the pain daily. Your most important relationship.
- **Technical evaluator** — Head of Eng / IT / Data. Cares about security, data handling, integration, and "build vs. buy." Can kill a deal; win them early.
- **End users** — the team whose work we'll embed with. Their buy-in determines whether the build actually gets adopted.

### Disqualifiers (walk away or de-prioritize)
- Wants a self-serve tool they run themselves — that's not us.
- No executive sponsor and no budget owner.
- Looking for a one-off proof-of-concept with no path to a real deployment.
- Process is too immature/undefined to map (sometimes a precursor engagement, not a no).

## 5. The Sales Process — Stages & Exit Criteria

The On Forward entry point is a **conversation** ("Your workspace begins with a conversation. Begin setup"). The motion is consultative — we're scoping a real engagement, not pushing a SKU.

| Stage | Goal | Exit criteria (don't advance until...) |
|---|---|---|
| **0. Prospect** | Identify ICP-fit accounts and a trigger | Account matches ICP; a plausible trigger or pain hypothesis |
| **1. Connect** | Earn the first conversation | Meeting booked with a champion or buyer |
| **2. Discover** | Map the pain and the real workflow | Confirmed pain, an owner, rough impact ($/time), and a workflow worth building around |
| **3. Scope** | Define a candidate engagement | Agreed problem area, success definition, stakeholders, and access to observe the work |
| **4. Propose** | Present the engagement & commercials | Buyer has the proposal; champion supports it internally |
| **5. Validate** | Address security/technical/legal | Technical evaluator satisfied; procurement path clear |
| **6. Close** | Sign | Signed agreement, kickoff scheduled |
| **7. Land & Expand** | Successful first build → next problem | First deliverable adopted; second use case identified |

### Qualification framework (MEDDIC, adapted)
- **Metrics** — what operational number improves, and by how much?
- **Economic buyer** — who can say yes to the spend? Have you met them?
- **Decision criteria** — what do they need to believe to choose us over the four alternatives in §2?
- **Decision process** — steps, timeline, procurement, security review.
- **Identify pain** — the specific workflow that hurts, quantified.
- **Champion** — who sells for us when we're not in the room?

A deal isn't real until you can fill all six.

## 6. Discovery — Questions That Work

Discovery is where this deal is won. Goal: surface a painful, quantifiable, mappable workflow and an owner who'll fund fixing it.

**Open the operation:**
- "Walk me through how [the function] actually works day to day — not how it's supposed to work, how it really happens."
- "Where does work pile up or get stuck? Where do people copy-paste between systems?"
- "Which tasks eat the most time but require almost no judgment?"

**Surface the AI history (and the skills gap):**
- "What have you already tried with AI? What happened?"
- "Where did that approach fall short?" *(Listen for: bought a tool nobody adopted, ran a course, hired and couldn't scale.)*
- "Who on the team can actually build and ship AI today?"

**Quantify the pain:**
- "How many people touch this workflow, and how many hours a week?"
- "If this ran 80% automatically, what would that free up — headcount, speed, capacity?"
- "What's the cost of leaving it as-is for another year?"

**Find the buyer and process:**
- "Who feels this pain most? Who owns the budget to fix it?"
- "If we built something that worked, what would have to happen for it to get rolled out?"
- "What's your security/data review process for an external partner?"

**The qualifying gut-check question:**
- "Are you looking for a tool your team runs themselves, or a partner who comes in, builds it around how you work, and gets it adopted?" *(If the honest answer is the former, we may not be the fit — better to know now.)*

## 8. Demo / "See It Take Shape" Flow

On Forward's promise is tangible ("See it take shape"), so the "demo" is less a canned product tour and more *showing the build come to life around their problem.*

**Recommended flow:**
1. **Anchor on their workflow** — replay what you heard in discovery: "You told me [X] eats your team's week. Let me show you what that looks like running differently."
2. **Show a relevant artifact** — a previously built agent/workflow analogous to their pain `[maintain a library of demo agents by use case]`. Emphasize it was built around a real operation, not configured from a template.
3. **Make it concrete and theirs** — "For you, the equivalent would be an agent that does [their specific task], plugged into [their systems]."
4. **Show the embed model** — explain *how* we'd learn their process (sitting with the team, mapping the workflow) so the build reflects reality. This is a key differentiator; don't skip it.
5. **Define the first win** — "The first thing we'd ship is [smallest valuable agent]. Here's roughly what that timeline looks like." `[INSERT typical timeline]`

**Demo principles:** Lead with their problem, not our capabilities. Show a working thing, not slides. Always tie back to "built around how *you* work."

## 9. Objection Handling

| Objection | What's really behind it | Response |
|---|---|---|
| **"Why not just use ChatGPT / an off-the-shelf tool?"** | Doesn't see why custom is worth it | "Those are powerful — but they're generic, and applying them to your actual workflow is the hard part most teams never get past. That's the skills gap. We build the applied system, around your real process, and make sure it's adopted." |
| **"This is expensive vs. a SaaS subscription."** | Comparing price, not value | "A subscription is a tool you still have to operationalize. We're comparing against the cost of the manual work continuing, plus tools that don't get adopted. What's a year of [X hours/week] of manual work costing you today?" |
| **"We'll just build it in-house."** | Has eng resources, or thinks they should | "Often the right long-term answer — and we transfer capability so you can. But hiring scarce AI talent and ramping is exactly the gap slowing everyone down. We bring it now, embedded, and hand it over as we go." |
| **"How do I know it'll actually get adopted?"** | Burned by past pilots/shelfware | "Because we don't build from assumptions — we embed and build around how your team actually works, with the end users, so it fits their day. That's the difference from a tool dropped on them from the outside." |
| **"What about our data / security?"** | Technical evaluator risk | "Fair and important. Here's how we handle data, access, and deployment: `[INSERT security posture, data handling, on-prem/VPC options, compliance]`. Let's get your security lead in the room early." |
| **"We're not ready / too busy right now."** | Low urgency or fear | "Totally hear it — and the irony is the busyness is often the manual work we'd remove. We can start small with one painful workflow. What would make 'ready' true for you?" |
| **"Send me some info / a deck."** | Brush-off or genuine | "Happy to — but our work is custom, so a generic deck won't tell you much. 25 minutes mapping one of your workflows will show you far more. Does [day/time] work?" |
